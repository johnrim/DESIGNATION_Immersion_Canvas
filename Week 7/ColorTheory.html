Color Theory

Color is an essential design element. It has an immediate impact on the viewer and can be used to elicit different emotions and create different moods. Color theories create a logical structure for how color can be used.

Examples of colors and the moods that they convey:

  • Red
    • Can be associated with love, romance, sensuality
    • Alternatively, can be used to connote fire, violence, and bloodshed
    • In China, red represents prosperity and good luck
  • Yellow
    • Often associated with brightness, happiness, sunshine
  • Blue
    • Introversion, sadness
    • Water, sky, expansiveness
  • Orange
    • Health, drive, pleasure, optimisim
    • In its muted forms, it can be associated with the earth and with autumn.
  • Green
    • Peace, tranquility, nature, vitality
  • Black
    • Death, absence, emptiness
    • Sophistication, exclusiveness, richness

Color Systems

  • Analogous colors: The colors are next to each other on the color wheel
  • Complementary colors: The colors are directly opposite of each other on the color wheel
  • Monochromatic: Different shades of the same color

Additive Color

Additive color works with anything that emits or radiates light.

  • Typically associated with RGB
  • White is the combination of all the colors, ie ffffff
  • Black is the absence of any color i.e. 000000
  • In hexadecimal, first 2 digits is red, the second 2 digits is green, and the last 2 digits represents blue.

Subtractive Color

Starts with white light and filters out certain wavelengths to give color.

  • Best represented by CMYK in print
  • RYB was used for mixing pigments in painting

How to Use Color

  • Create a color palette for a design: I like to stick with no more than 4-5 primary colors in the palette. This is a general guideline, not an absolute rule!
  • Once you've picked your set of primary colors, you should create a palette for your secondary colors.

Note: Even if you decide to go with a monochromatic or muted color palette, you still need accent colors for contrast and highlighting important elements like call-to-actions.

  • Saturation of a color can be used on infographics to suggest density
  • Brightness of a color can be used to communicate visual hierarchy and states
  • Different hues of a color is great for communicating different sections or different button or interaction states
