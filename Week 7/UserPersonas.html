Brief

User personas are realistic representations of your key audience segments for your product/service. The goal of personas is not to represent every possible audience member but rather to address the major needs of the most important user groups.

Key points:

  • Personas are based on user research and data
  • Personas are realistic but fictional characters
  • Personas can be used for usability testing and product design
  • Personas helps everyone on the team to be on the same page
  • Personas should only be 1 page
  • Personas should address what the user needs, wants and limitations are
  • Typically, there will be 3-5 personas created for a project

Elements of a user persona

  1. Demographics:
    • Name
    • Age range
    • Gender
    • Marital/relationship status
    • Income level
    • Highest education
    • Occupation
    • Location
  2. Picture
  3. Archetype:
    • User categorization
    • Personality type
    • Quote
  4. Psychographics:
    • Goals
    • Needs
    • Frustrations
    • Concerns
    • Motivations
    • Interests
    • Influences
    • Beliefs
  5. Behavioral patterns:
    • How often do they use the product?
    • Are they using the product by choice or by requirement?
    • When do they use the product?
    • Where do they use the product?
  6. Skills:
    • Technical expertise
  7. User environment:
    • Devices
    • Platforms
    • Software

Empathy Maps

Empathy Mapping helps us consider how other people are thinking and feeling. Typically, research notes are categorized based on what the research interviewees were thinking, feeling, doing, seeing, and hearing as they engaged with your product. It helps your team zoom out from focusing on behaviors to consider the users’ emotions and experience as well.

Empathy maps vary in shapes and sizes, but there are basic elements common to each one:

  • Four quadrants broken into “Thinking,” “Seeing,” “Doing,” and “Feeling.”
  • Covered in sticky notes
