What is Usability Testing?

Usability Testing is a user-centered design technique used to evaluate a product by testing it on users.

Elements of a Test Plan

  1. Purpose
  2. Testing Objectives
  3. User Profile
    • Identify the target audience
    • Demographics
    • Technical expertise
    • Existing customer or new user
    • Should match your user persona
  4. Methodology
    • Will this be an in-person test or a remote test?
    • How will users be recruited?
    • What moderation technique will be employed?
  5. Task List
    • What is the user scenario that you are trying to test?
    • What is the task(s) the user is being asked to perform? Tasks should represent the most common user goals or most important for conversion or center around the most complex task.
    • Typically, participants will perform a set of 5 to 10 tasks within a 60-90 minute session.
  6. Environment & Equipment
    • Will there be a computer? If so, is it a laptop or desktop or both? Is it a Mac or PC? What is the hardware on those computers?
    • Will the test occur in a lab or in a classroom or in the user's home?
    • Will the test be remote?
    • Is this a mobile test? If so, what kind of mobile device will be used?
  7. Evaluation Measures
    • Are you having users rate the aesthetics on a scale?
    • Are you using the net promoter score?
  8. Report Contents and Presentation

Conducting a Usability Test

Moderation Techniques

There are a few methods you can employ:

  1. Concurrent Think Aloud: The goal is to encourage participants to keep a running stream of consciousness as they interact with a product by having them think aloud while they perform a task.
  2. Concurrent Probing: Requires that as participants work on tasks—when they say something interesting or do something unique, the researcher asks follow-up questions.
  3. Retrospective Probing: Requires waiting until the session is complete and then asking questions about the participant’s thoughts and actions. Researchers often use RP in conjunction with other methods—as the participant makes comments or actions, the researcher takes notes and follows up with additional questions at the end of the session.

Best Practices

  1. Treat participants with respect and make them feel comfortable.
  2. Open the user test by telling the user that you are not the designer.
  3. Do not jump in and help participants immediately and do not lead the participant
    • Don't explain the interface to the user. This defeats the purpose of the usability testing
  4. Remain neutral – you are there to listen and watch.
    • Never interrupt the user when they are giving feedback
    • Avoid defending the design when the user is explaining why something is unclear or confusing
  5. Take notes
  6. Record the session using audio or video
  7. Measure both performance and subjective (preference) metrics.
    • People's performance and preference do not always match. Often users will perform poorly but their subjective ratings are very high.
    • Performance measures include: success, time, errors, etc.
    • Subjective measures include: user's self reported satisfaction and comfort ratings.

Example Usability Test Session

  1. The facilitator will welcome the participant and explain the test session, ask the participant to sign the release form, and ask any pre-test or demographic questions.
  2. The facilitator explains thinking aloud and asks if the participant has any additional questions. The facilitator explains where to start.
  3. The participant reads the task scenario aloud and begins working on the scenario while they think aloud.
  4. The note-takers take notes of the participant’s behaviors, comments, errors and completion (success or failure) on each task.
  5. The session continues until all task scenarios are completed or time allotted has elapsed.
  6. The facilitator either asks the end-of session subjective questions or sends them to an online survey, thanks the participant, gives the participant the agreed-on incentive, and escorts them from the testing environment.
  7. Try to test with at least 5 different participants.
